Deleting code is the way to go!

I spotted a fun tweet earlier today that brought up one of the happiest moments in my coding career.

The celebration is over a couple Spring geeks having ripped out a HUGE and unnecessary bunch of bloat known as Node.

Now before you laugh and howl (or cry and shout), this has nothing to do with Node. Instead, it’s about what’s needed vs. what’s cool. We ALL have a tendency to write using our favorite toolkits. It’s hard to shake!

In fact, I recently wrote an article about When to NOT use Spring Boot. This problem we software developers have is universal. I like Spring Boot, I tend to use it everywhere I go. Node devs like Node, they tend to use it everywhere they go.

A veteran software developer must learn to rise above such strong emotional desires to run with our favorite toolkit, and ask the question, “What is BEST in this scenario?” And oftentimes, the corollary question, “What will the readers/users of this most likely know/not know?”

Back when we published all the guides at https://spring.io/guides, we put forth a guiding vision that each guide in and of itself would be self-contained. A guide wouldn’t be much of a guide if someone had to read three other side articles to finish it. And it also wouldn’t help if they had to complete some other guide first.

No, none of that would fly. So we made the decision to make each guide self-contained, and to explain all the key bits by the end. Someone giving away their lunch break to try and grow their Spring knowledge needed to be rewarded with a feeling of accomplishment. And that’s exactly what we did.

At the top of this article I mentioned “one of the happiest moments of my career”. That would be the time before I joined the Spring team. I took over someone else’s back office application. Something that had cost literally MILLIONS in invested time (six previous developers had apparently handled it). And it was NON-OPERATIONAL.

This app, meant to digest about 300 monthly invoices and spot discrepancies, had been gobbled up in “patternization”. (Patternization is a word I just made up to indicate usage of patterns exceeds value of patterns.)

This app’s purpose for the accounting department to go through statements, spot problems, and issue corrections so we could not pay so much. And we’re MILLIONS of dollars. Every month.

The app simply didn’t work.

These invoices were spreadsheets and the app had a beautiful abstraction for rows and columns. But it simply didn’t work.

These 300 invoices actually had about five special formats (not 300), so they weren’t unpredictable. But it simply didn’t work.

The requirements were almost unchanging. But it simply didn’t work.

For something so small in scope, so simple in requirements, and so slow to change, it was strange (to me) that it didn’t work.

When I picked it up, I vowed to not change a single line of code without writing a test case first. (The app had next to NO test cases). And so I got busy.

I wrote painstaking test cases. And edited code. Another test case, another patch. Another test case, another tweak. And soon, in the span of 1–2 months, I built up a suite of tests.

And I reached a point of awareness where I KNEW that spreadsheet abstraction served no purpose. And I also knew I could rip it out without anything being damaged.

And so I did.

I deleted a chunk of code, and suddenly the whole app leaped forward in speed. I was running this test suite at least once a day. It would often take 30 minutes to execute. It would also creep up toward an hour, and I’d spend a day tracking down what needed to be fixed, be it tests or production code.

The spreadsheet abstraction wasn’t the only thing I ripped out. I yanked other stuff that my test suite exposed as unnecessary.

I also started meeting with program managers, the users, and others. I started to implement features they needed. I also kept writing tests.

Once, I ripped out a HUGE chunk of code…

…and break half the tests. Three hours later, it still didn’t work. So I scrapped all the changes I’d made for the day and went home worn out.

Next day, I started fresh, and moved in a different way. The tests had told me that I had gone too far, and gave me the confidence to back up and start over.

Merrily moving along over the next six months, I was meeting weekly with the users. The application was working. The users were actually doing their jobs much faster. We would get together, brainstorm new ideas which I’d collect in a slideshow. One page per “idea”.

And I’d go back to my desk, translate these slides into test cases (one test case per slide) using the lingo we had spoken in the meeting. And I’d code the corresponding solution.

Tests STILL took about 30 minutes to run. But then something happened.

My manager called me into his office. He was preparing for the next quarterly meeting with the VP of engineering and was putting together his report.

He showed me the lines of code they ran, and pointed at my application. Instead of having an exponential GROWTH curve, it instead had an upside-down PARABOLA shape. Indeed, I had REDUCED the total amount of code in this application by a HUGE amount.

And the application did more than EVER. And…

…it worked.

The application simply worked. It met the needs of the users. They were getting through monthly invoice reconciliation at a slim and trim speed. Considering these users operated on overhead, this had caught the attention of upper management.

And they couldn’t be more pleased. Total cost of maintenance was DOWN while throughput was UP.

All because of a solemn promise I made to myself.

Don’t fix anything without writing a test case to capture it.
