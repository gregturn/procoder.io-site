Are you ready to commit yourself to being a pro coder in 2025?

January 5th, 2025
Imported from Medium

There is a keen fact that separates hobbyists and amateur enthusiasts from career-seeking pro coders: hobbyists can stop and start whenever they wish. A hobbyist can carry out a tutorial, try out a new stack, or experiment with a new library recently released. This is true.

But a pro coder does all these things for the sake of their clients or the shop they work for.

There are naturally consequences to this.

We cannot stop and start as we wish.

While you should take breaks and re-energize suitably, you need to seek to move the needle. Drive things forward. Iterate on either a piece of code, a continuous integration job, or update the reference documentation even when we don’t feel the passion.

Sometimes you’ll be tired.

Sometimes you’ll be frustrated.

And sometimes no one around you will have the answer you seek. You must find it yourself.

A hobbyist in such a situation would set the issue on the shelf and go do something else. Perhaps another beginner’s tutorial. Or perhaps something else entirely.

A pro coder squints his or her eyes, feels a desire a PASSION to defeat the problem laid bare, and pushes forward!

We must learn all that we can learn

There is something called the arrival syndrome. A situation where you feel as if you’ve “mastered” some topic and that there is no more to learn.

The arrival syndrome is horrible and something that can take the most capable, most skilled people and let them decay into nothingness.

There is always more to learn. New stacks. New toolkits. New protocols. New WAYS of doing some of the old things.

I recently dig some digging into Cloudflare’s Zero Trust offering. I was interested in the possibility of accessing my RAID array’s internal Dropbox-equivalent FROM ANYWHERE ON THE INTERNET.

But I didn’t want to open any ports if possible. Zero Trust is a “newer” way of building a VPN. I have enjoyed Cloudflare’s other offerings, so I dug into Zero Trust.

I began reading docs. Configuring my home router in ways I didn’t know were possible. Gearing up my iPhone and my MacBook Pro to interact with Zero Trust. I’m not convinced it’s for me.

But the point is, I have learned new things despite having been in this industry for 27 years!

My favorite Christmas presents this year were three books. Two of them are Jack Reacher novels I haven’t read yet, the third being THE PIRATES OF MANHATTEN by Barry Dyke. I’m excited about all three.

If you’re still reading, there is hope for you. If you haven’t read for years, it could be a sign. Let me know in the comments when you last read something and if you’re bold enough, what it was.

In software development, the world changes rapidly. Learning is MUST. What you learned in college is probably obsolete if we’re talking about tech. But critical if what you learned include HOW TO LEARN.

AI is upon us. I have already began dipping into different AI tools to lend me assistance in content creation. I don’t use them to write the words you are now reading. But I sure as heck have leveraged it to help me create introductory content, Q&A post-session questions, graphics, even videos. We have no clue where this is going, but the potential for a revolution as great as the Industrial one is immense.

Don’t. Stop. Learning.

We must hone our craft with our tools

Have you ever wondered by doctor and dentists offices are called “practices”? It’s because while these people learned critical skills in medical school, it’s their daily work in which they PRACTICE their medical and dental arts.

As pro coders, we must practice as well.

This is why I am challenging you to explore working with at least THREE SPECIFIC IDEs this year. If you need a suggestion, I would recommend IntelliJ IDEA, VS Code, and Eclipse.

If you don’t use Java, but instead another stack entirely, you’ll have to make a different choice.

But the point is to pick up new tools and take them for a run. And to make it a real, sincere, learning experience, I suggest you commit to at least six weeks with each one.

The point is to learn how to navigate code. How to compile code. How to test code. How to “find” stuff. How to make the IDE serve you, and not you serving it.

You probably won’t learn all the hot keys. That’s okay. You’ll probably be less efficient than you’d like. Again, that’s okay. The point is to learn. To grow. To expand your knowledge.

Because when we practice learning, we become better at learning. And when it’s against something that truly challenges us, then the experience can a stronger effect on us.

Leave me a comment and tell me you’re going to commit. You’re going to try some new IDEs!

Are you ready for 2025? It’s here. It’s ready to throw you all kinds of curve balls.

Maybe you don’t have everything together. Maybe you don’t know all the answers. I’m not asking for that.

All I want you to do is be ready to GROW this year! Be ready to EMBRACE new things. Be ready to truly become a PRO CODER!

— Greg
