What books should I buy to become a Spring developer?

I wrote an article over nine years ago talking about this, and it’s woefully due for an update.

The struggle with answering this, is that when I was learning the ropes of software engineering, my circumstances were different. For one thing, SUN Microsystems was still a company! And Java was a newborn technology people didn’t fully grok.

But understanding the spirit of new grads and fledgling developers seeking to get a jumpstart on their coding careers, I have built up this list of recommendations I think will benefit you.

Look at all these people LOVING Spring books!### 1 — My books!

Well, duh! What author isn’t going to recommend their own?

Pre-order your copy today at https://trnq.st/book!On a more serious note, I recommend my own books because I strive to offer hands-on coding examples along with practical solutions. I also like to point out choice you have to make and the tradeoffs that involves.

Engineering is fraught with tradeoffs. There is rarely a hard rule that doesn’t have some exception. It’s important to understand the costs and benefits in making choices. And if there’s there one thing I can bring to the table, it’s experience with Real World(TM) systems.

I worked on a 24x7x365 operational system for ten years. I came in after hours to fix things if they broke. I once came in three times on the same night. Yikes! I built tools to me stave off outages. That sort of crucible hones your ability to build apps as well.

And so, I try to put some of that experience into my books.

2 — Spring in Action by Craig Walls

There’s a reason this teammate and personal friend has the biggest selling title at Manning. He is a real Spring pro! And he was an expert on Spring when I was but a wee lad. He’s not older. He’s just been in the trenches of Spring development from the early days.

Grab the latest version of Spring in Action at https://trnq.st/o2oa!And he knows LOTS of ins and outs. His content is gold.

And it’s entertaining. Having written probably more titles than anyone I know (including me), he has honed his writing style to where you can have fun AND learn valuable concepts.

And he’s always making updates to his tomes of knowledge. Maybe that sounds like a negative, but it’s really a positive. At first blush, you may feel like he’s simply trying to make an extra buck. In truth, you KNOW that he will have a new book in the next 1–2 years with the latest features. Spending $30–40 every other year is a reasonable price for some rock-solid information.

3 — Whatever your UI solution is

What is your team using to build its UI? React? Vue? Angular? JavaFX? Swing? Griffon?

If you even KNOW what Swing of Griffon are, leave me a comment below!

Whatever your team is using, it is of keen benefit to understand it even if you aren’t directly tasked with building it.

That’s right.

Even if you aren’t part of the UI-building team, understanding what they are building will be valuable. It will make YOU more valuable to understand it. To be able to contribute to it, here and there. And, on occasion, to chip in a pull request.

I’m no expert at JavaScript. I’m more of a Padawan. But this has helped me work with our UI experts and also interact with those on our team that are NOT.

If THIS is what YOU’RE building, I’d LOVE to see it!### 4 — Whatever your data store solution happens to be

Where are you storing all your data? Dimes-to-donuts it’s a relational database. Postgres? MySQL? MariaDB? Oracle?

Even if you’re using something like MongoDB of Apache Cassandra, it’s of benefit for you to go grab be best-selling title on that datastore.

Boning up your skill son Postgres or MongoDB will help you layer your application on top of that. Understanding tables or document structures will give you the ability to discuss deep-seated solutions that leverage your datastore with maximum power.

And don’t grab some foolish ANSI SQL book. That’s ridiculous. SQL itself has too many gaps and is spread across too many standards for those books to REALLY help you.

If your team is using Postgres, grab a Postgres book and start reading! Interoperability isn’t as valuable as leveraging that datastore to the hilt. The odds your management will decide to ditch Postgres and switch to MariaDB are pretty much nil.

### 5 — BONUS TIP

There’s one ADDITIONAL tip I want to offer.

It’s not something you’ll find in a book or a handout or a LinkedIn post.

It’s this — create a Google Document and start taking notes. Daily notes. Write down what you’re working on. Write down what you’re struggling with.

Create an engineer’s journal.

You see, when I started my career long ago, before Google was even a thing, I had a small notebook. I wrote down daily activities. Routine actions. Stuff I would do.

Journaling, “old school”That fell by the wayside, but I recently upgraded that concept and started writing myself a personal journal in a Google Document that is just for me.

And it has, more than anything, served as a way to gauge my activity. It gives me instant feedback on my personal development velocity.

And, because I write down everything I work on (not just completed code), it is GREAT at helping me beat back the beast of Imposter Syndrome. At a glance, I can see everything I’ve done in the past 4–5 days. And simply SEEING my efforts, research, and completion, means I no longer have to rely on my MEMORY to REMEMBER what I’ve accomplished.

I can SEE what I’ve accomplished. And I go to bed at night much more satisfied that I’m making the difference that I originally sought by becoming a software engineer.

And don’t forget — books are a means to learn some knowledge. But they don’t contain everything you need to grow as a developer.
