When to NOT use Spring Boot!

Spring Boot is awesome. Spring Boot is great. It’s the bee’s knees. So why would you NOT want to use it? That’s what we’ll cover in this article.

1 — You already have a working application

Something we developers have a problem with is being handed an already-working application. It’s loaded with twisted paths of logic and coded with gnarly patterns. Oftentimes, this ball of mud before us screams “just rewrite me!”

And many-a-times, this is a mistake.

What we don’t realize is that this code contains a lot of information. A lot of knowledge. Many vital decisions, meetings, and analyses are tied up in this precious little hunk of code.

Simply rewriting it implies that not only can we write its immediate function better. We can handle all those nasty corner cases that had to be dealt with. Customer issues. Vendor wrinkles. The whole of it.

So before you reach for the almighty Spring Boot in the hopes of rewriting something written with a “lesser” toolkit, consider that maybe you’d be better off UNDERSTANDING the thing first.

Spend time learning its test kits. Make some additions and adjustments by first writing new tests. Once you’ve walked a few miles in that app’s proverbial shoes, you may just have a better understanding of how Spring Boot can help.

And maybe you won’t have to REWRITE it so much as ADAPT it to the world of Spring Boot.

ADAPT usually beats REWRITE!### 2 — You could use…something else!

We certainly love writing those Spring MVC controllers, crafting those Spring Data repositories, and locking it down with Spring Security policies. It’s fun to wield such power.

But is this really the best use of your time?

I recently wanted to roll my own “bit.ly”. A web site that would me create short links using my OWN domain. I had briefly looked up the cost of going bit.ly itself and paying to use a custom domain.

Nope.

*To code it myself or NOT to code it myself…that is the question!*Instead, I did a little extra research and opted to craft a lightweight Wordpress site with the Pretty Links plugin! The truth is, most of my time was hunting down the right domain and finding a domain provider that sell it to me. I found the official provider of .st domains in São Tomé, did some hunting, and found I could easily register https://trnq.st for just 29 EUR a year!

With that setup, I configured Cloudflare DNS and had a Wordpress site rolled and operational within 48 hours.

Now imagine trying to code a site that did A) random URL slugs, B) optionally custom URL slugs, C) metrics tracking, D) and the UI to go with it. Yikes!

Some things are easier to use already-baked solutions, and frankly NOT worth your personal time. The same can be said if you’re building a blog site or CMS system.

What’s that I hear? Isn’t https://spring.io a custom Spring Boot application? Yes it is. We initially crafted that back in 2014 and gave it a facelift just a couple years ago.

And it has required a bit of maintenance. Not because Spring Boot is trouble. But simply because ANY CUSTOM SITE YOU BUILD will have Ops-related issues, maintenance demands, and other issues you can’t ever foresee.

When you reach for someone else’s site-baking toolkit, be it Wordpress or Nuxt or whatever, you are taking advantage of literally MILLIONS of hours of development time from often huge communities. Why presume you can roll that in your off hours?

And even if you have to pay 29 EUR/year + whatever your hosting provider costs, it’s still a LOT cheaper than the hours YOU’LL have to spend getting it up and running and then sustaining it!

3 — Spring Boot just doesn’t fit

We all like to use our favorite solution everywhere, right? But sometimes, things just don’t fly. If you’re part of a big team, then odds are, more than one application is being built.

One of the backend systems MAY be using Spring Boot. But another one ISN’T!

And what if that OTHER system is built by the gulp JavaScript team!

“I am so scared!”All joking aside, the JavaScript team MAY be using Node to do everything. They may use it for the frontend AND the backend.

And that’s okay. You can’t get your fingers into everything. And if we’re talking about a huge team/contract, then you need to focus on your area of influence.

Getting bent out of shape because another team is NOT using Spring Boot isn’t the way to go.

The other piece in all this is that JavaScript-based systems have an entire WORLD of tools: build systems, deployment tools, IDEs, and management stuff.

Waltzing in and suggesting to a team of JavaScript pros that they need to cast it all aside isn’t likely to resonate.

So simply…let it go.

### 4 — It’s not your decision

Sometimes, when you join a new team, and they’ve already made a choice on which way to move forward, the opportunity to pick has passed. And it’s your job to implement it!

Perhaps this comment I’ve just made sounds a little rude? Well, if you’ve joined a team that just finished three weeks of meetings, discussions, and email threads in order to form a plan, then it may be YOU that is rude. That is, if you’re suggesting they drop EVERYTHING and pivot to Spring Boot.

If you want to be a professional software developer, then one piece of that is communication. And professionals don’t go out of their way to argue and push. Instead, try to learn what decisions were made. Invest time in groking the choices made. And write the best code you can!

Just write the best code you canBecause things change. People roll off projects. Some day, YOU may be the lead developer, and you’ll be faced with Yet Another Decision to make for either a new app, or an authorized rewrite of this one.

And that is the time to apply you’ve learned about the current app along with what you’ve gleaned from Spring Boot Learning, and pitch your best idea (which, hopefully, is Spring Boot!)

If YOU’RE facing down a decision to Spring Boot or NOT to Spring Boot, then leave me a comment.
